Double Helix
Divya's Review
Double Helix is a scientific memoir written by James Watson, who won a Nobel Prize in 1962 for his work with DNA which is highlighted in this book. This book comes from Watson’s perspective which led to tinged of bias. Watson’s character seemed harsh from his portrayal of Rosalind Franklin, but he did concede(in the epilogue) to the fact that she was treated unfairly. Overall, this book provided a glimpse into the “race” to discover the structure of DNA. Although Watson’s descriptions seemed to bash others, such as Rosalind Franklin, it gives valuable insight into the politics and clashes in the scientific world. One specific event that struck me as extremely odd, and in my opinion deteriorated the character of Watson was when Watson listened to a speech given by Rosalind Franklin and decided that he didn't need to take notes because he could recall everything from memory. Franklin was lecturing on groundbreaking ideas in the DNA world, and since Watson was researching this field it was important that he take notes on her speech. I think if Watson had just acknowledged that he made a mistake by not noting Franklin's speech, then his honesty would be admirable. Instead, Watson chose to tell Crick incorrect facts from Franklin's speech because he had forgotten the specifics. This event reminded me that you can learn something from anyone and everyone. It is important to remember that nobody is perfect and there is always room for improvement. Life is filled with endless learning possibilities, and pride should not stop anyone from pursuing these possibilities. Another event that I found interesting was that all the scientists that were working to find the structure of DNA were rivals. I assumed that everyone would share their research with their colleagues to discover the structure of DNA sooner. But after reading Double Helix, I can see that they treated one another as competitors. I understand why the scientists each wanted to accomplish this themselves, but I think there is still a need to stay ethical and true to the scientific field. When Peter Pauling approached Watson and Crick and told them his father, Linus Pauling, would publish a paper about DNA (most of the mystery regarding DNA was solved in this paper), Watson and Crick noticed a major, but an easily fixable mistake, but they didn't warn him. In fact, they urged him to publish the paper. At this point, these scientists had all worked very hard on DNA research, and this became more than just rivalries between colleges/countries. I believe that Watson and Crick should've put aside their competitive attitudes and helped a colleague. It is also important to note that this book depicts the scientific community in the 1950s. During this time, women were expected to either stay home or have traditionally female jobs such as telephone operators and secretary positions. Though Watson’s portrayal, it is obvious that Rosalind Franklin wasn’t treated the same as others. In fact, one of Watson’s close friends stole her research and gave it to Watson. In the epilogue, Watson mentions that he now knows his behavior towards Franklin was wrong, and he alleges that she was a brilliant scientist. It was very mature of Watson to admit he wronged Franklin, but I think books like this are essential to see how scientific research has evolved. All in all, this book gave an unfiltered view of scientific research through Watson’s eyes; And explains how a trailblazing scientific discovery was made. And although reading it in current times highlights the difficult research nature of the past, it is obvious Watson is not sugar-coating any instances, and all events seem raw and honest.
